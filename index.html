This research project examines how data infrastructure is reshaping modern governance systems. As governments worldwide implement biometric identification systems, digital health passports, and centralized data platforms, fundamental questions emerge about citizenship, sovereignty, and inequality.

We investigate the socio-technical assemblages that constitute contemporary governance regimes, focusing on how digital identity systems mediate access to rights, services, and resources. Our research spans multiple geographies and examines the political economy of data extraction, the privatization of public infrastructure, and the differential impacts on marginalized communities.

Through ethnographic fieldwork, policy analysis, and critical data studies, we trace the material consequences of algorithmic governance. Our work contributes to urgent debates about technological sovereignty, data justice, and the future of democratic accountability in an age of ubiquitous surveillance.

Who are we?

We are an interdisciplinary research collective bringing together scholars from science and technology studies, political economy, anthropology, and critical data studies. Our work is grounded in long-term ethnographic engagement with communities affected by data-driven governance initiatives, combined with rigorous analysis of policy frameworks and technical systems.

Based across multiple institutions and continents, we collaborate with civil society organizations, policy makers, and affected communities to produce research that is both analytically rigorous and socially engaged.
